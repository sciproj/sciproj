The Water Cycle

  1. Water evaporates from bodies of water such as lakes, rivers, & oceans
  2. Water vapor condenses into clouds
  3. Precipitation occurs. Precipitation is rain, snow, hail, & sleet that falls from clouds. This precipitation can land in one of three general places: a body of water (back to step 1), on the ground, or on a mountain, where if it isn't already snow, it will become snow or ice.
  4. If the precipitated water became runoff (landed on the ground) it will either go into a body of water (back to step 1) or it will become groundwater through infiltration. However, if the precipitation became snow or ice, it will eventually melt and become runoff itself.
  5. One of two things will happen to groundwater: It will either A, seep into a body of water through groundwater discharge (back to step 1), or B, be sucked into a plant through it's root system.
  6. Finally, water that has been sucked up by plants will evaporate through transpiration (back to step 2). Transpiration occurs due to pores called "stomatoes" in a plant's leaves letting water evaporate out of the plant, similar to sweating in humans.
Source

How the Water Cycle Works

The various processes involved in the water cycle are mainly governed by some combination of gravity and sunlight, the following list will list each process in the water cycle along side a descriptpion of how that process is mainly governed by the two aforementioned occurances (sunlight & gravity)

  • Evaporation-Evaporation is caused by sunlight heating up water enough that some of it evaporates
  • Condensation-Condensation is infact one of the only aspects of the water cycle that, as far as I'm aware, is not governed by sunlight or gravity
  • Precipitation-Precipitation is caused by gravity pulling particulates of rain down from the clouds when the water in the drop has condensed around a mote of dust or insect
  • Infiltration-Infiltration is caused by gravity pulling runoff, puddles, etc. into the ground when it can, causing that water to become groundwater
  • Runoff-Runoff is caused by gravity pulling water on the ground down various slopes until it reaches a body of water
  • Melting-Melting is caused by sunlight heating up snow or ice to the point where it melts into a liquid
  • Transpiration-Transpiration is essentially plants sweating, the sweat is then evaporated (and in fact can even be caused by) sunlight

Change and Movement of Water in the Atmosphere

Warm air rises and cold air falls, when warm air rises the pressure where the warm air was drops and when cold air falls the pressure where it's headed increases. Of course air that's under pressure will want to escape to areas of lower pressure, causing wind. However, due to the Coriolis Effect that wind does not travel in a straight line, but a curved one.

Interconnected Ocean Currents

As illustrated in the above picture, there is a cycle of interconnected ocean currents commonly reffered to as "The Great Ocean Conveyer Belt". This belt is caused by thermohaline (thermo=temperature, haline=salinity) circulation. Thermohaline circulation occurs deep within the ocean and allows it to act as a sort of conveyer belt which absorbs, stores, & redistributes vast amounts of the Sun's heat around the world, without it, places at the same lattitude on the globe would have temperatures that were generally similar. However, at the moment there can be vast differences between the temperatures of places at similar lattitudes. For example, there is almost a 20 degree gap between the average annual temperatures of Norway and Manitoba, Canada. The belt transfers water from the Pacific Ocean to the Atlantic Ocean in a warm, shallow current and vice-versa in a deep, cold current that is South of the warmer one. Starting in the central Pacific the belt travels past the North coast of Australia and around the tip of Africa. As it goes up into the Atlantic it becomes the Gulf Stream and when it passes Western Europe the warm water evaporates, simultaneously cooling itself off and warming the atmosphere at that point, this is responsible for Western Europe's relatively warm climate. When the water cools off it increases in salinty and attains a higher density, this causes it to sink thousands of feet beneath the surface, slowly travelling South in the abyss of the depths, only surfacing after up to 1,000 years.

Influences on Weather and Climate

Coriolis Effect

The arrows in the above picture show the path an object would take if you were standing at the equator and threw it towards one of the poles, assuming you throw in an exactly straight line and the object travels far enough you will note that instead of traveling in a straight line it curves to the right in the north hemisphere and left in the south. This is because different parts of the Earth travel at different speeds. This occurs because no matter where you stand that point will have traveled in a circle around Earth's axis in 24 hours, the problem is that at certain points that circle is a lotlarger than others. For example, a point on the equator has to travel around the circumfrence of the Earth (~25,000 miles) in 24 hours making that point travel at a speed of ~1,040 mph, while a point a foot away from the north pole only has to travel ~6 feet in the same amount of time making it move at only ~0.00005 mph. Of course, when an object is thrown perpendicular to the throwers direction of movement, the object retains some momentum in the direction the thrower is moving and therefore curves in that direction. This is how the coriolis effect works as it means that when an object moves towards a part of the planet it appears to curve in the direction the planet spins in, and when thrown towards an area that spins slower it will appear to curve the opposite direction. The coriolis effect doesn't only affect thrown objects, it also affects the direction in which hurricanes spin, the direction large-scale winds travel in, etc.

Atmospheric Circulation

There are belts of wind that encircle the planet at various altitudes, which are organised into 3 cells in each hemisphere, the cells are mirrored around the equator, as demonstrated in the above image. However, the positions of these cells are actually based on temperature, so whenever I say equator in this section (including in the previous sentence) I really mean the thermal equator, whose position varies seasonally as it is not based on geography, but, as the name implies, on temperature, meaning that it moves North when it's summer in the Northern hemisphere, and South when it's summer in the Southern hemisphere. For convenience, however, most diagrams show the cells in the position they're in on the equinox, the only time the thermal equator and geographic equator match. Furthermore, whenever air rises in these cells it results in a low pressure system and it's falling results in a high pressure system. You may notice that the arrows in the diagram that represent surface-airflow are curved istead of straight as one would expect, this is due to the previously mentioned Coriolis Effect. Also, I will only be discussing the Northern cells here, if you want information regarding the southern cells simply replace the word "north" with "south" and vice-wersa.

Hadley Cell

Air in the Hadley Cell rises at the equator and falls 30 degrees of lattitude to the North. Warm air, warmed by the temperatures in this area, flowing South hits other warm air flowing the opposite direction from the cells Southern counterpart at the equator, and rises. Once the air reaches a certain height, it cools and increases in density, falling 30 degrees of latitude to the north before beginning to flow towards the equator, starting the cycle yet again.

Ferrel Cell

The Ferrel Cell is between the Polar and Hadley Cells and occurs, not because of a natural process like the other two cells but simply because of adjacent air being moved by other cells. It is in fact somewhat comparable to an eddy.

Polar Cell

While not as warm as the air at the equator, the air that's 60 degrees above the equator is still sufficently warm and moist to rise, after which it begins to move polewards before finally reaching the pole and falling. The falling is caused by the cold at the pole swiftly cooling off the air and causing it to increase in density before being shoved back southwards to where the cycle began.

Weather Prediction

Meteorologists cannot perfectly predict the weather, it like almost all future events cannot be perfectly predicted by a human. Put simply, meteorologists predict the weather by comparing what's currently happening with what has happened in the past, for example, if there are 10 days when certain conditions are recorded and on 9 of those days a certain outcome ocurred, then a meteorologist would predict that there is a 90% chance that on a day with those conditions, the certain outcome will occur. This of course is done on a much larger scale in the real world. In order to record previous weather conditions and determine new ones meteorologists use a wide variety of tools, such as barometers, thermometers, and anemometers. However, they also sometimes attach these tools to large ballons called weather ballons, as depicted in the above image, in order to record the atmospheric conditions higher up.

Causes of Climate Change

As you can see in the above image, heat from the sun bounces off the Earth and back into the atmosphere, some of that heat bounces back, and some goes straight through. This is caused by gases called greenhouse gases (GHG) which are named due to the fact that they act somewhat like the walls of a greenhouse in that they bounce back some of the heat that hits them. As you may have noticed in the bottom-left corner of the image above, without the greenhouse gases in the atmosphere the Earth would become very cold. However, humans are currently pumping immense amounts of greenhouse gases into the atmosphere, this could eventually result in an increase of several degrees farenheit, you may be thinking that a few degrees won't make much of a difference, but the latest ice age was a meager 12 degrees colder. Infact, an increase of just one degree would raise the sea level enough to displace tens of thousands of people living on islands or the coast. Due to rising sea levels caused by global warming, by 2045, 64,000 homes in Florida will be at risk of flooding evry other week, and that number could jump to a million by 2100, according to this webpage. Agriculture also has an effect on climate change, not only is it a significant user of land and fossil fuels, but it also directly contributes to greenhouse gas emmissions through pratices such as rice production and the raising of livestock, as is detailed here. Also, accordig to the IPCC, agriculture is one of the top three contributors to climate change (Source). The concrete industry is responsible for over 5% of man-made CO2 emmissions, a relatively high number (Source).Also, the Co2 pproduced in the creation of standard concrete is 410 kg/cubic meter according to this.

Greenhouse Gases

Two characteristics of gases determine their greenhouse effect, one of them is GWP (Global Warming Potential) and their atmospheric lifetime. The GWP is a measure of the radiative effect of each unit of gas over a specified period of time. This is expressed relatively to the most common greenhouse gas, CO2. An amount of gas with a high GWP wil warm the Earth more than the same amount of CO2 over a given amount of time. The atmospheric lifetime determines how long a gas will remain in the atmosphere before it is disposed of by natural processes (such as chemical reactions). A gas with a long atmospheric lifetime can exert more influence on global warming tha one with a shorter lifetime (assuming they have equivalent GWP's). The table below contains both of these values for the various greenhouse gases according to the Fourth IPCC Assesment Report (AR4), information from which, I found here.
